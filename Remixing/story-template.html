Dissecting Trailers of Oscar Favorite Films

How the trailers of Silver Linings Playbook, Amour, and Lincoln look like when breaking down every cut.

By HUIYI LIU

Disclaimer: The following article is original from NYT.

Published on September 14, 2019

The first ever movie trailer was shown in November 1913, and since then, releasing trailers is one of the most common and effective marketing tools to advertise for the upcoming films. In recent years, with the rapid expansion of internet, many previously released trailers resurface on video sites such as Youtube, as newly released ones are also uploaded online and remain accessible for future viewers. Trailers appear to be more prevelant and powerful than ever. Audience can easily find and watch these short edited clips at any time to get an idea of the tone and mood of the film.

There are several conventions when it comes to the making of a film trailer, which are preeminently established by the Hollywood film industry. A common trailer follows the three-act structure composition that highlights segments of the film's beginning, middle and ending. The last act often consists of visual montage with moments of the sotryclimax and emotion outburst to convey maximum appeal.

The following article will look at the trailers of three films that were nominated for Best Picture at the Academy Awards. By visualizing the breakdown of every cut of these trailers, this analysis aims to dissect the the makeup of the trailer in relation to the narrative progression of the respective film.

“Silver Linings Playbook” follows the standard model for trailers and largely align with the order of the film itself. Even though, as it is shown in the grpah, at the beginning of the trailer there is a shot from the film's ending part, it does not pertain to any important inforamtion, but rather, it is a establishing shot of the house that the main character lives in, which somewhat has no narrative significance. The trailer also feature clips that are not in the full-length film. Beside graphics of titles and credits, the "not in film" part contain some deleted scenes from the film, an other common practice in movie trailer.

Clips from the beinnging of the film take up the majority of the trailor, follows by the middle, and little of the ending is revealed. There are 106 cuts in the 148-second of trailer, and the relatively fast cut set the tone for tension and drama.

Unlike the previous example, the trailer of "Amour" did not care much about following the narrtive order of the film. Instead of displaying moments of emotion outburst, the trailer gradually lays out an emotion buildup by cutting back and forth between different parts of the film. The middle part of the film has the longest runtime in total, follows by not-in-film clips, which are all titles and credits.

On the other hand, the longer shot length in the trailer corresponds to the stylistic choice of long takes in the film. The trailer is also relatively shorter, perhaps a reflection of its less commercial nature.

The trailer of "Lincoln" perhaps is the most bizarre of all. The alternation between the beginning/middle part and the ending part in the trailer showcase the background context of the film while at the same time emphasizes on the tensions. It is more about setting the mood and tone rather than giving a synopsis of the film in a chronological order.

It is also worth mentioning that the part that takes up the longest runtime in the trailer is actaully from non-film clips. Besides some large text fonts of the title and credits, several not-in-film clip is actaully a "blackout" scene with only black background on the screen, and it last for several seconds. What a great way to build up suspense.
